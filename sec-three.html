    Section 1.3 Numerical Data

    Objectives

    • Introduce basic concepts and correct terminology about Statistics.
    We examine data through graphs/plots and numbers/statistics. We provide some definitions which are relevant in this chapter.

    Remark 1.3.0.1.

    We have differen kind of plots/graphs to summarize data.
    • Scatter plot This kind of plot provides a case-by-case view of data for two numerical variables.
    • Dot plot This is a one-variable scatterplot. It is the most basic of displays.
    • Histograms plot It has different interpretations, but, in general, it is a binned version of the stacked dot plot. It also can be a frequencies plot. We will discuss more about this in the following sections. With this plot, we can know more about data density When data trail off to the right in this way and has a longer right tail, the shape is said to be right skewed Otherwise, we will have left skewed If data sets show roughly equal trailing off in both directions are called symmetric
    • Box plot.
      1. Draw the median
      2. Draw a rectangle to represent the \(50/%\) of the data
    • Mapping plot. This kind of plot works to show different parameters/variables in a map. It implements different colors and their intensity to associate get up or down for any variable.

    Let us explore some mathematical concepts.

    Remark 1.3.0.4.

    • Mean AKA Average It is one of the most popular way to measure the center of a distribution.
      \begin{gather} \overline{x} = \sum\limits_i^n \frac{x_i}{n}\tag{1.3.1} \end{gather}
      It is the sample mean, where \(x_i\) represents the \(i-\)th observed value.
    • Population mean and Sample mean can be calculated trough the same way, using the \(\mu = \sum\limits_i^N \frac{x_i}{N}.\) Notice, the population mean is represented by \(\mu.\)
    • Weighted mean. Essentially, mean and weighted mean are same, except that the last one has an impact if the number of observations change.
      \begin{gather} \text{Weighted mean of } x_i= \frac{\sum\limits^{n}_{i=1} w_i x_i}{\sum\limits^{n}_{i=1} w_i}\tag{1.3.2} \end{gather}
      where \(w_i\)‚Äôs are the weights, and \(x_i\)‚Äôs are the observations. Notice the eqs. (1.3.1) and (1.3.2) are same if in the last one \(w_1=w_2=w_3= \dots = w_n = 1\)
    • Mode. It is the prominent peak in the distribution (sometimes it is identified as the highest rectangle) in the histogram. Besides, it is the value with the most occurrences in the data set. But this definition has some issues, such as, it is uncommon to have repetitions in the data set. On the other hand, the data set can have more peaks, e.g. one mode (unimodal), two mode (bimodal), or several modes (multimodal).
    • Standard deviation. It is a measure of variability. Mathematically, the standard deviation is
      \begin{equation*} \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N-1} \sum\limits^{N}_{i=1}(x_i - \overline{x})^2} \end{equation*}
      and it roughly describes how far away the typical observation is from the mean. Usually, one standard deviation means the probability that every observation will be within one sigma band is \(68.5\%.\) Two standard deviation means the probability that every observation will be within two sigma band is \(95.5\%\) and so on. Check the figure‚Äâ2‚Äâ (check the website blog‚Äâ3‚Äâ). Notice The deviation is the distance of the observation from its mean, namely, \(x_i - \overline{x}\text{.}\) For instance, \(9.5 - 10 = -0.5\) is the deviation.
    • Variance. It is a measure of variability. It is called as the second central moment of the sample.
      \begin{align} \sigma^2 &= \frac{1}{N-1} \sum\limits^{N}_{i=1}(x_i - \overline{x})^2\tag{1.3.3} \end{align}
      Note the eq. (1.3.3) is the definition of variance. The variance is the average squared distance from the mean. Notice the variance symbol is \(\sigma^2.\)
    Additionally, the sigma measure usually means that
    • the \(68.5\%\) of data usually was within \(1\sigma\) of the mean.
    • the \(95.5\%\) of data usually was within \(2\sigma\) of the mean.
    • the \(99.75\%\) of data usually was within \(3\sigma\) of the mean.
    • the \(99.995\%\) of data usually was within \(4\sigma\) of the mean.
    • the \(99.9995\%\) of data usually was within \(5\sigma\) of the mean.
    We will add more relevant concepts those help in statistical analysis.
    Median denotes the concept to split data in half (fig. Figure¬†1.3.0.5). In other words this concept refers the \(50^{\text{th}}\) percentile. The median has two options
    1. Even number of observations. In this case we will calculate the average.
    2. Odd number of observations. In this case we will have only one observation that splits the data into two halves.

    Remark 1.3.0.6.

    • Interquartile range (\(IQR\)). It is the total length of the box plot. It is a measure of variability.
    • First quartile (\(Q_1\)). It is boundary to indicate that the \(25\%\) of the data fall below this value.
    • Third quartile (\(Q_3\)). It is boundary to indicate that the \(75\%\) of the data fall below this value.
    • Whisker.
      1. max \(\frac{3}{2}IQR\)
      2. upper \(Q_3+\frac{3}{2}IQR\) from \(Q_1\)
      3. lower \(Q_3-\frac{3}{2}IQR\)
      \begin{gather} IQR = Q_3 -Q_1\tag{1.3.4} \end{gather}
    • Outliers.
      1. identify bias in the distribution.
      2. Establish data entry errors.
      3. Reveal potential properties of the data.
    Let us check other statistical concepts. The following concept is relevant to analyze data and its influence under changes.

    Remark 1.3.0.7.

    • Robust statistics. Two concepts constitute the Robust statistics: Median and IQR. These have little effect when there are extreme observations.
    • Transformation. It is a recalibration of data through a mathematical function, this usually works to rescale the data a make easier to build data models. Some transformation examples are \(x\to\log(), x\to\ln(), x\to\sqrt{x},x\to x^{-1}\)
